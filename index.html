Black Hole Time Dilation

Written by Izzy Perez for Black Holes 2030

Introduction

There is a way to time travel! Well, kind of. It’s not about jumping into the past to change history or avoid a speeding ticket. Instead, Einstein predicts that if you travel faster or sit near something more massive than someone else, you will age less than them. Time moves differently depending on speed and gravity.

You might recognize this effect in one of the most iconic space science fiction films of our time: Interstellar. In one of the most memorable scenes, astronauts on a humanity-saving mission land on a wave-covered planet orbiting a supermassive black hole. When they return to their ship just a few hours later, their crewmates, who stayed further from the black hole, have aged decades. Praised for its use of real science (thanks to Nobel Prize-winning physicist Kip Thorne, who consulted on the film), this scene is not just fiction. In this article, we’ll dive deep into the gravitational well and explore the facets of this version of time travel.

Speed Time Dilation

When you move really fast, you will age more slowly compared to someone standing still. The time dilation equation describes this effect:

$$\Delta t = \frac{\Delta t_0}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$

\(\Delta t_0\) is the change in time you experience traveling near the speed of light.
\(\Delta t\) is the time someone who is standing still would measure time passing.
\(v\) is your velocity as a fraction of the speed of light.
\(c\) is the speed of light.

Let's say that you travel for 10 seconds at 90% the speed of light, 22 seconds would be experienced by the person standing still, watching you.

It is one thing to write out the equations and have the math be sound. It is another thing entirely to actually observe the effects you just calculated. As crazy as this seems, we use speed time dilation. Particles that decay quickly, such as muons or lithium ions, can be accelerated by a partical accelerator to speeds close to the speed of light. To an outside observer (the scientist), the particle takes longer to decay. This gives scientists more time to study them.

Gravitational Time Dilation

It turns out, gravity also causes time dilation. Imagine a marble sitting on a stretched sheet of fabric. It causes a small dent in the fabric. A black hole is so massive that it sinks the fabric down to a point infinitely deep. When you are next to a black hole, you are sitting in this well. Spacetime is stretched and curved around you. This stretching slows down the passage of time, so someone far away will see you aging more slowly than themselves. The equation describing the time dilation caused by a massive object is this:

$$\Delta t_0 = \Delta t \sqrt{1 - \frac{2GM}{rc^2}}$$

\(\Delta t_0\) is the time you experience near the black hole,
\(\Delta t\) is the time someone far away would measure,
\(M\) is the mass of the object,
\(r\) is the distance to the object’s center, and
\(G\) is the gravitational constant.

This time dilation effect has also been tested by scientists. In the paper "Resolving the gravitational redshift across a millimetre-scale atomic sample," researchers showed that even over just a few millimeters in height, clocks tick at slightly different speeds. Even Earth's weak gravity causes a measurable difference in time because one is closer to Earth and experiences slightly more gravity than the one clock above it. Even with masses the size of Earth, gravitational time dilation can be observed.

Combining the Two

When you hover near a black hole, gravity pulls so strongly that you would eventually fall in unless you are moving fast enough to stay in orbit. To maintain a steady orbit, you need to travel at a speed dictated by Kepler's laws, just like the moon orbits Earth. But near a black hole, orbits get extreme. You end up moving at fractions of the speed of light while sitting deep inside the gravitational well.

Now, two forms of time dilation are happening at once: one from your speed and one from the gravitational pull. It is not as simple as just adding the effects together, because spacetime itself is curved in a way that mixes both speed and gravity into a single formula. Solving the Schwarzschild metric gives an equation that accounts for both effects:

$$\Delta t_0 = \Delta t \sqrt{1 - \frac{3GM}{rc^2}}$$

This is the equation I used for the time dilation simulation you see here. Try experimenting with it. You will notice that the closer you get to the black hole, the more extreme the time dilation becomes. Can you reach a time dilation factor of 60,000, like the one seen in Interstellar? No! Anywhere close enough for that would put you inside the innermost stable circular orbit, or ISCO. The ISCO is the point where the gravitational well becomes so steep that a circular orbit is no longer possible. If you cross it, you spiral into the black hole.

So what gives? Is Kip Thorne a fraud? (Spoiler: no, he literally won a Nobel Prize.)

Interstellar and Kerr Black Holes

It turns out there is a way to shrink the ISCO of a black hole and orbit closer, experiencing even more time dilation. The trick is to make the black hole spin. A spinning black hole is called a Kerr black hole.

Kerr black holes wind up spacetime and cause something called frame dragging. This is where spacetime itself is twisted around in the direction of the black hole’s spin. Frame dragging vastly complicates the equations of spacetime around the black hole, making the math extremely difficult. However, one thing we know for sure is that spinning black holes shrink the ISCO. This lets you orbit closer and possibly experience gravitational time dilation as extreme as what is shown in Interstellar.

Did Kip Thorne work out all the complex math behind Kerr black holes? No. It would have been too complicated to solve by hand. However, he used real black hole physics, such as the fact that spin shrinks the ISCO, to design a scientifically accurate situation where extreme time dilation could realistically happen.

Conclusion

Black holes create some of the most extreme time dilation effects in the universe. Near a non-spinning black hole, time dilation increases as you get closer, but it is limited by the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO). To push time dilation even further, a spinning Kerr black hole shrinks the ISCO, allowing orbits much closer to the event horizon. However, even today, no one has fully solved the time dilation equation for an object traveling near the speed of light, deep within a gravitational well, while also experiencing frame dragging from a spinning black hole.

The math is incredibly complex and difficult to do by hand. But we live in the age of advanced computing power. If someone puts their mind to it, we might finally solve this equation to make even more accurate simulations, and to gain a deeper understanding of how spacetime behaves in the most extreme environments.
